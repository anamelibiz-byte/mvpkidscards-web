🏀 Tips for Parents

5 Ways to Share Your Athlete's Card Link Without Being "That Parent"

By the MVP Kids Cards Team • May 15, 2025 • 4 min read

We know — asking people to buy stuff for your kid feels awkward. Nobody wants to be the parent whose texts are all fundraising links. Here are five ways to share your MVP Kids Card that feel natural, non-pushy, and actually work.

The biggest fear most parents have when they start sharing their athlete's card link is coming across as desperate or preachy. You don't want to be the GoFundMe person who sends passive-aggressive reminders. You don't want to guilt your coworkers into buying something they don't care about. And you definitely don't want to spam your family group chat.

Here's the good news: MVP Kids Cards makes this easier than traditional fundraising, because you're not asking for a donation — you're sharing something people actually want. That changes the whole dynamic. But delivery still matters. Here are five approaches that work.

Best for family

01. The "I Made Something Cool" Text

This is the easiest and most natural opener. Instead of leading with the fundraising angle, lead with the card itself. You're not asking for money — you're sharing something you made that you're proud of.

Try something like this

"Hey! I just made an official trading card for [athlete's name] — it turned out SO cool. Thought you'd want to see it. You can grab one here if you want: [link]. No pressure at all!"

The key phrase: "No pressure at all." Say it and mean it. People are far more likely to buy when they don't feel obligated.

Best for social media

02. The Athlete-First Post

On Instagram or Facebook, lead with your athlete — their story, their highlight, something they accomplished recently. Make the post about them, not the card. Add the card link almost as an afterthought, like you just remembered it exists.

Caption idea

"So proud of this kid. [Name] had an absolutely incredible tournament last weekend — 3 goals, team MVP. She works so hard and it shows every single game. ❤️ We made her an official trading card if anyone wants one — link in bio. 🏆"

When the post is clearly about celebrating your child, the card purchase feels like participation in that celebration — not a transaction.

Best for team parents

03. The Group Chat Show-and-Tell

Post your card in the team's parent group chat as a "look what I just did!" moment — not a sales pitch. The card itself is visually impressive enough that other parents will immediately want one for their own athlete.

Drop something like

"Just got [Name]'s card back — they turned out incredible, I'm obsessed. Made them through MVP Kids Cards. Goes on sale [date] if anyone wants one! 🃏"

This approach also opens the door to the whole-team fundraiser model — where every family creates a card and the whole team benefits. We've seen teams raise enough to cover an entire tournament season this way.

Best for grandparents

04. The Personal Text with a Story Attached

Grandparents, aunts, uncles, and close family friends respond best to personal, direct communication. Skip the group blast and send individual texts to your closest people. Include a detail about the athlete that only someone close to them would know or care about.

Personal touch example

"Grandma, I made the cutest thing for [Name]. She has her own official trading card now with her photo, her stats, and a little bio (she insisted I include that she wants to play D1). I'd love for you to have one — here's the link: [link]. They're only $18 and she would absolutely lose her mind knowing you have one."

The detail about the athlete's dream or personality is what sells it. You're not pitching a product — you're sharing a piece of who your kid is.

Highest conversion

05. Show the Physical Card First

If you order physical cards (and you should — they're the best version), show them to people before you post the link. At games, at practice drop-off, at family gatherings. Let people hold it, look at it, turn it over. The tactile experience converts far better than any text message.

Once someone holds a real, professionally printed trading card of a kid they care about, they almost always want one. Then share your link naturally: "You can order on the website — I'll text you the link."

Pro tip: Keep 2–3 cards in your bag or car. You'll be surprised how many people at sporting events will ask where you got it after they see you holding one.

What NOT to Do

A few things that consistently backfire:

  • Sending multiple follow-up reminders. One share is enough. Two is borderline. Three or more and you become "that parent."
  • Posting in large, impersonal groups. A church Facebook group, a neighborhood app, a random community board — these audiences don't know your athlete and don't have reason to buy. Focus on people who know and love your child.
  • Framing it as charity. "We're trying to fundraise for Sofia's season, anything helps!" positions you as asking for a favor. "Sofia has her own trading card now!" positions you as sharing something exciting. Same link, completely different energy.
  • Setting a dollar goal in your post. "We're trying to raise $500" signals desperation and makes people feel like their $18 won't matter anyway.

Lead with pride in your athlete. The card is proof of something amazing they've done. People don't need to be convinced to celebrate a kid they love.

The Bottom Line

The families who have the most success selling cards are the ones who genuinely believe in what they're sharing — because they should. An MVP Kids Card is genuinely cool. It celebrates your athlete, it's beautifully made, and it's something the buyer will keep. Your job isn't to sell it. Your job is to show people it exists. The card sells itself.
