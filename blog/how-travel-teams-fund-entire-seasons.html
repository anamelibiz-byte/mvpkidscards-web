How Travel Teams Are Using MVP Kids Cards to Fund Entire Tournament Seasons

By the MVP Kids Cards Team • April 8, 2025 • 5 min read

Coaches are discovering that when every family on the team creates an MVP Kids Card, the whole team wins — enough to cover travel, hotels, and tournament entry fees. Here's how it works, the math behind it, and how to pitch it to your team parents.

Travel sports is expensive. Depending on the sport and level of competition, a single travel team season can cost a family anywhere from $2,000 to $10,000+ when you factor in registration fees, tournament entry, uniforms, equipment, coaching, hotels, and transportation. And that's per athlete, per season.

Most clubs run some combination of individual fundraising and team-wide efforts — candy sales, car washes, corporate sponsorships, GoFundMe campaigns. Most of these approaches are inconsistent, embarrassing for athletes, or require significant time from volunteer parents.

A small but growing number of coaches and team managers have found a better way. They coordinate a team-wide MVP Kids Cards campaign — every family creates a card for their athlete simultaneously — and the collective fundraising from 12 to 20 families can cover a significant portion of the team's season costs.

The Math: What Does a Full-Team Campaign Look Like?

Let's run the numbers for a typical travel soccer team with 15 athletes. Each family creates a Standard Edition card (serial-numbered out of 25) and prices it at $18.

Why Coaches Love This Model

The team-campaign approach solves a problem that coaches and team managers deal with every season: how do you run a fundraiser that parents actually participate in, without turning it into a full-time job?

"I've done candy bars, wrapping paper, and car washes. This was the first fundraiser I've run where parents were excited. Everyone wanted their kid to have a card."

— Soccer coach, Austin TX

The key dynamics that make team campaigns work:

  • Social proof: When every family on the team is doing it, it normalizes the effort. Parents aren't self-conscious about sharing because everyone else is sharing at the same time.
  • Team pride: There's something powerful about a roster of 15 athletes, each with their own professional card. It builds team identity and something families can be proud of together.
  • Peer motivation: When one family's card sells out first, others notice. The competitive spirit that makes great athletes also motivates families to sell.
  • Collective reach: 15 families sharing to their respective networks represents an enormous combined audience that no single family could match.

How to Run a Team Campaign: Step by Step

1

Introduce the concept at a team meeting or in the parent group chat

Keep it simple: "We're doing a team trading card fundraiser. Every athlete gets their own professional card, families sell them to friends and family, and we use the proceeds toward the season." Give families 1–2 weeks to set up their cards.

2

Coordinate a "card reveal day"

Set a date — ideally a game day or practice — where every athlete on the team brings their physical cards. The reveal creates buzz, photo opportunities, and team bonding. Athletes holding their own professional trading cards alongside teammates is a moment families won't forget.

3

Launch sharing simultaneously

Coordinate a "launch day" when all families post and share their card links at the same time. The collective social media moment creates more visibility than staggered individual shares.

4

Track and celebrate milestones

Update the team chat as families sell out. Celebrate the first sellout, the team's collective total, and any standout stories. Keep the energy up through the campaign period (usually 2–3 weeks).

5

Allocate proceeds

Decide in advance whether families keep their proceeds individually (recommended for most teams) or pool a portion toward a shared team fund (works well for clubs with shared expenses like tournament fees or equipment).

Coach tip: Make it part of the season narrative. "This season we're going to tournament X, and we're funding it ourselves — every athlete on this team is going to have their own trading card." Athletes who are bought-in to the campaign sell far more cards than those who are passive. Make it a team goal, not just a parent chore.

The Ripple Effect: What Happens After the Cards Are Out

The fundraising is just the beginning. Teams who have done card campaigns report a persistent secondary benefit: the cards keep getting shared and talked about long after the campaign ends. Grandparents show them to friends. Coaches keep them in a frame in their office. Former teammates trade them years later when they reconnect.

One soccer club in Houston has done card campaigns for three consecutive seasons. The coach told us that graduating players from the club's older teams come back to get younger athletes to sign their cards at tournaments — a tradition that started organically and now happens every season.

That's what happens when fundraising creates something worth keeping.
