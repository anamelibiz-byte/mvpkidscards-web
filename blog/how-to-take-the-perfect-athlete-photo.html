How to Take the Perfect Athlete Photo for Your Card

By the MVP Kids Cards Team • May 3, 2025 • 3 min read

Your child's photo is the most important element of their trading card. The stats, the design, the edition type — all of it is secondary to a great photo. Here's how to get one with nothing but your phone and good timing.

We've seen thousands of athlete photos come through MVP Kids Cards, and there's a clear pattern: the cards that look incredible almost always have a great photo. Not a professional photo — just a photo taken with intention. These tips will help you get there in one afternoon without spending anything on equipment.

The Golden Rule: Action Over Posed

The single biggest difference between a card that looks like a keepsake and one that looks like a school portrait is movement. Sports cards look like sports cards because they capture athletes doing athletic things. A posed, stiff portrait is fine for a yearbook. For a trading card, you want energy.

The best cards we've seen feature athletes mid-sprint, mid-jump, mid-swing, or mid-dribble. The second best? An athlete who is clearly in their element — in their kit, on their field or court, looking like they belong there.

✓ Great photo choices

  • Action shot from a game or practice
  • Mid-sprint or mid-jump moments
  • Ball skills in motion (dribbling, kicking, throwing)
  • Natural pose in full uniform on the field/court
  • Power stance with equipment (bat, glove, stick)

✗ Photos that underperform

  • Stiff posed portraits in civilian clothes
  • Group team photos (too small, too busy)
  • Blurry or low-light shots
  • Photos taken from very far away
  • Selfies or funny faces

Lighting: The One Thing That Makes Everything Better

You don't need a photography studio. You need good light. Natural light, specifically, makes phone cameras perform dramatically better than even expensive lighting equipment in the wrong hands.

Golden Hour Is Your Friend

The hour after sunrise and the hour before sunset produce the warmest, most flattering light of the day. Shadows are soft. Colors are rich. Skin tones look healthy. If you can get your athlete outside in their kit during golden hour, you will get an incredible photo.

Overcast Days Are Underrated

Overcast skies act like a giant softbox — they diffuse sunlight evenly across your subject with no harsh shadows. Many professional photographers prefer overcast conditions for portraits. If the sky is cloudy, it's a great day to shoot.

Avoid Harsh Midday Sun

Direct midday sun creates unflattering shadows under eyes, noses, and chins. It also causes squinting. If you're shooting midday, look for open shade — under a canopy, on a shaded part of the field, or with the subject turned away from the direct sun.

Quick tip: shoot with the sun behind you, not your athlete

Position yourself so the sun is over your shoulder, lighting the front of your athlete's face and body. If the sun is behind them, they'll be backlit and their face will be dark. Simple, but it changes everything.

Getting the Shot on Game Day

Game day is the best time to capture real emotion — celebration, focus, determination. Here's how to maximize your shots at a real game or tournament:

  • Arrive early. Warm-up routines often produce great action without the chaos of gameplay. Athletes are focused, moving, and in their element.
  • Use burst mode. On iPhone, hold down the shutter. On Android, hold down or use the dedicated burst setting. Burst mode gives you 10–20 frames per second to choose from, dramatically increasing your chances of catching the peak moment.
  • Get low. Shooting from a slightly lower angle (crouching or kneeling) makes athletes look powerful and dynamic rather than small. Even three feet lower changes the visual dramatically.
  • Anticipate the action. Focus on your athlete before the play develops. Start shooting before the peak moment. By the time you react, the moment has passed.
  • Check your focus. On most smartphones, tap the screen on your athlete's face or torso to lock focus before you shoot. If the background is sharp and your athlete is blurry, the card will look amateurish.

Resolution and Cropping

Our platform requires a minimum of 800×800 pixels for uploaded photos, but we strongly recommend larger — ideally 2000 pixels or more on the shortest side. Modern smartphones (2020 and newer) all capture photos well above this threshold, so this usually isn't an issue as long as you're not using heavily compressed screenshots or downloads.

When cropping for your card, aim for the athlete to fill the frame. We recommend cropping tightly enough that your athlete occupies most of the image — from the top of their helmet or head to about mid-thigh. Head-to-toe shots with lots of background tend to make athletes look smaller than they are. Tighter crops feel more like professional cards.

Don't over-filter your photo

We see a lot of heavily filtered Instagram photos come through — boosted saturation, heavy contrast, dramatic vignettes. These can actually hurt the card because our design templates are already styled, and double-processing creates muddy, artificial-looking results. Upload the cleanest, most natural version of the photo and let the card design do its work.

The Pre-Shoot Checklist

Before you shoot, confirm:

  • Athlete is in full uniform (no street clothes)
  • Uniform is clean and numbers are visible
  • Phone storage is clear (burst mode fills up fast)
  • Phone camera is on highest quality setting
  • You're shooting in good natural light (not harsh midday sun)
  • Burst mode is turned on
  • Background is clean (avoid busy parking lots, crowds behind the subject)
  • Athlete's face is unobstructed (no helmet visor, no shade across eyes)

One More Thing: Let Them Be Themselves

The best trading card photos we've seen share something in common: the athlete looks like themselves. Not like they're posing for a photo — like they're playing. The fierce expression, the determined focus, the joy of a goal celebration — these are the moments that make cards feel real and alive.

Don't over-direct. Set yourself up in a good position with good light, get your camera ready, and let them play. The shot will come.
